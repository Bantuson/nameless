## Default — act on this

Default approach: Step one: write a chord progression to build the vocal loop over — when you catch yourself humming ideas over the progression, take it as the sign the progression is inspiring and you're ready to record. Comp the clearest, closest-to-pitch takes — they are the best starting place to build from — then use the pitch editor to redraw the notes you sang, like MIDI; treat manual note redrawing as the most important step, and after manual correction add a light autotune just to catch any remaining wild notes. Don't crank autotune and reverb to mask a bad vocal — it won't hide it. For the pitched-up R&B vocal effect (Frank Ocean on Ivy / White Ferrari), use varispeed rather than a simple pitch shift. Finally, make a quick beat and test the vocal in context to confirm it actually works.

### varispeed — 1 source(s) agree
Slow the project's tape speed down, record at that speed, then speed it back up so the voice sits higher — use varispeed's Speed and Pitch mode for this technique. Recording slowed-down means the key is lowered and the pace is slower, making the part doubly easier to sing. Tune the varispeed amount to your own voice — here, four semitones down works best.
